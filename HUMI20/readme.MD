## Comparing the Greeks with their neighbors
### 30/30
### Prompt: 
Based on the this week's readings, the film, and the PPTs, in what ways was the Bronze Age Greek world similar to the cultures around it and how was it different?  Please share your thoughts with the class, first as a post, and then as two responses to the posts of your peers.  I will keep the discussion open until Sunday evening.

### Answer:
In this essay we compare and contrast the Bronze Age Greek cultures with those around it. The Bronze Age Greek cultures consist of three different civilizations: Cycladic , Minoan, and Mycenaean. The cultures around the Bronze Age Greek include those in Mesopotamia, Anatolia, and Egypt. These cultures were similar in their usage of bronze, their social hierarchy, polytheistic religious systems, and the development of writing systems but differ in how their religious impact their power structure and the source of their economic foundation- river vs sea. 

These cultures are similar in their usage of bronze, their social hierarchy, polytheistic religious systems, and the development of writing systems. Bronze metallurgy was developed in Mesopotamia to produce military and agricultural tools in 4000 BC (“The First Civilizations in the West” 16) and “came to Greece and its islands around 3000BC” (Pomeroy et al. 23). Later, during Hyksos invasion, the technology was introduced to Egypt (“Old and New Kingdom Egypt” 25). For example, Mycenaean use bronze to create daggers (“The World of the Greeks”  20; Pomeroy et al. 38 F1.9c) and plate armor and boar’s-tusk helmet from Dendra in Argolis (Pomeroy et al. 50 F1.12a). Bronze Age Greeks also developed a "sociopolitical hierarchy” (Pomeroy et al. 24). In Crete, a few families own most of the wealth, privilege and status (Pomeroy et al. 32). Similarly, Egyptians also had Well-defined social classes during both Old Kingdom (“Old and New Kingdom Egypt” 21) and New Kingdom (24). In Mesopotamia, ruling classes were usually defined by their military prowess, with the assistance from religious classes to govern free commoners and slaves (“The First Civilizations in the West” 17). All of these cultures worshipped a pantheon of multiple gods and goddesses. Egyptian worship of Osiris, Isis, and Re (Colum; “Old and New Kingdom Egypt” 12-19). Mesopotamian worship gods like Anu, Ishtar and many others (“The First Civilizations in the West” 11-13). In ancient Greece, the Cyladic figures are likely fertility goddesses (Pomeroy et al. 26). Minoan performed communal rituals in deep caves, as well as in public courtyard toward a goddess, with her “consorts” male gods (33-34). One such Goddess is Minoan Snake Goddess (“The World of the Greeks” 13). Mycenaean adopted the sacred symbols and cult objects of the Minoans. The tablets from Mycenaean Cnossus and Pylos denote around thirty Mycenaean gods and goddesses, who were forming the famous major Greek gods later (Pomeroy et al. 49). For the development of writing systems, in Mesopotamian Sumerians first experimented with pictographs but later invented “wedge-shaped” Cuneiform to record trade, astronomy, agricultural, and calendar systems on clay (“The First Civilizations in the West” 19-21). In Egypt, Hieroglyphs, a mix of pictographic, supplemented with symbols representing sounds and ideas, were used for writing since 3200BCE (“Old and New Kingdom Egypt” 9-11). In Greeks, Linear A was used in Crete (“The World of the Greeks” 12) and Linear B was used in Mycenaean culture (21). 

For the differences between them: First, Greeks never “put temples and the priesthoods at the center of political and economic power, as in Mesopotamia and Egypt” (Pomeroy et al. 23). Second , while both Egypt and Mesopotamia are land based development while Greeks culturally are centered at the Agnese sea and sea travel is a norm in their society. Agriculture in Egypt is heavily dependent on the seasonal flooding of the Nile River to fertilize their land (“Old and New Kingdom Egypt” 5). Similarly, the flooding of Tigris and Euphrates rivers created the “fertile crescent” which vitally supported agriculture in Mesopotamia (“The First Civilizations in the West” 6-7). However, that is different in Greek! Geographically, Greeks are ocean people and heavily depend on the Agnese sea instead of rivers (“The World of the Greeks” 2). 

We can see Greeks are similar to their surrounding cultures in Egypt and Mesopotamia but yet unique. They all start to develop the use of bronze, live in a class system based social hierarchy, worship polytheistic deities, and record by using writing systems. But their religions have different positions in their society and their economic sources were built from sea rather than from the flooding of rivers.

#### Three Fujins, 1995, Hung Liu
In Three Fujins, 1995, Hung Liu framed the work by the two thick orange vertical analytical lines on both sides to contain the three female figures. The two orange doors, decorated with white outlines, are visually assertive and steady. The thin white outlines of the orange door strengthen the thickness of the door visually (“The Formal Elements - Line”). Liu skillfully applied Rule of Third in her composition, notice the door only covers the top to third of the sides and leave the lower one third to some white objects rendered expressively. Two horizontal implied lines were formed in the lower portion of the painting, one on the edge where the shoes touch the ground and one with the thick lighter color of the sitting bench. Inside the framed center, Liu painted the background black and subdivided horizontally to three equal spaces, each occupied by one female figure. Liu uses her famous "veil of tears" to create her expression (“The Formal Element of Line: Thinking Critically”). The "veil of tears" technique is conducted by loading a lot of linseed oil on the top and then employing gravity to drag the oil paint down. These gravity drag create natural expressive lines. Liu controlled the density of the oil to create different heaviness of expression of each line. Some oil drags over the eyes of the female figures on the right, creating an illusion of “tear”. It casts an illusion of the whole painting crying silently with the freedom that only gravity can liberate. 
There are many patterns and variations rendered in the image. The three female figures dress all the same, with all similar kinds of dress and sitting posture. They all hold a fan in their left hand and place their right hand on their lap. There are three bird cages hanging in front of them. These are the repeated patterns. However, if we observe carefully, the one in the center holds a circle hand fan while the two on two sides each hold a folding fan. While all of their right hands are on their lap, the center one has her palm faced up and the two on each side has their palm faced down with a different hand gesture. The black borders illustrated on their clothes are also slightly different, indicating these are not uniform but rather a type of dress worn by their class but each of them still have freedom to express their personal taste on the choice of  border of the cloth. 
I believe Liu frames the image with two doors to ground the image and provide us a context of the situation (“The Formal Elements - Line”). The thick orange door implies a formal setting and visually corners the viewers into the setting inside a Chinese palace. The black background, on the other hand, hinted that they are sitting right in front of an entrance under the sun. The painter uses that blackness to inform us they are just barely outside the inner palace with all the darkness we cannot see what happens, but yet still within the social system structured by the two orange doors on the sides. They sat outside the darkness but yet under constraints. 
The blurred and highly expressive black background visually took place in the center and occupied the top two thirds of space. They were also mirrored from the reflection on the ground. The ground reflection lets the viewers believe the floor is clean and shining. Liu uses the black background to contrast the white cloth and skin color of the three figures in the foreground to pop their significance. The blurred vertical lines in the black background invite the viewers to imagine what is inside the room. They cast a shadow of the mythical life of these three female figures experienced daily and attract our curiosity. They functioned as the landscape behind Mona Lisa (“Art History and Methods of Analysis?”) painted by atmospheric perspective (“How to do visual (formal) analysis in art history” 4:07) rendering and dragging the viewers into the dark palace and similar to the sfumato style used by Leonardo da Vinci in The Virgin of the Rocks (“Tools of Visual Analysis”). Liu here really created a new variation of Chiaroscuro.

#### Part 1- The Visual Elements of the Hall of Bulls, Lascaux, France (c. 15,000 BCE.)
Hall of Bulls is a large prehistorical cave painting that resides inside Lascaux Cave in the Dordogne region of southern France. This photograph only shows a small portion of the painted wall of the Hall of Bulls. The medium is ochre pigment painted on white limestone. In the photo, we see the artist paint large animals from the floor to the ceiling of the cave, covering the whole wall. In the center right of the wall, there is a half wall tall round hole entering another chamber. It is a painting of various wild lives in a natural setting, presumably recorded what the artist observed and memorized before they entered into this dark cave. The artwork is in a naturalistic rendering style. The wall is mostly white while the floor is grey and the ceiling are different bulbous forms shaded by various ochre pigment and black charcoal. 
The animals painted on the wall are overlapping in several layers. The largest are three horned bulls heading from the left to the right, two on the right of the entrance hole and the leader passed. Their heads and bodies are illustrated with contour lines, but their legs are rendered black. Grey shading applied to the lower portion of their stomach to build up the volume of their appearance. They are portrayed in profile view. The front portion of the right most bull is painted overlapping the tail of the center bull, utilizing the overlapping perspective to show it is closer to the viewers. The first and last bull are depicted as focusing on their walking but the second one raises its head and with a slide forward moving posture, creating a sense of chasing the left one moving faster. 
The second group is a group of horses painted smaller on the left. They are shaded with brown solid color and heading to the right. Their smaller scale hints to the viewers they are located further away than the three bulls. One is overlapping with the stomach of the left most bull and making the stomach of the bull transparent. The position hinted it is entering into the entrance hole. 
Two red bulls seem to render on the right edge of the wall, a middle size one overlapping a smaller one, depicted behind the two front legs of the right most bull. Their scale and positions are illogical and awkward because they are smaller but placed lower than the larger one, creating a violation of linear perspective if it is rendering both animals in the same scene. It may suggest the cross-temporal rendering of the theme from two different times overlapping in the same view, similar to the multi-exposure filming technique invented in the 19th century by contemporary photographers to “capture the moment”. Therefore, the larger white bulls and the two solid smaller bulls may represent animals in two different time frames, unified in the same landscape setting, crossing the time constraints. 
Similar to the overlapping issues between the white and red bulls, there are two smaller black horses rendered in front of, or displayed lower than, the middle size brown horse. All three of these black horses are run and head right, similar to the large brown one. The middle size brown horse and the center smaller one in the front both have very long necks, hinting they may be giraffes but not horses. However, it is hard to tell. 
Four deer are illustrated even smaller in scale. They are all rendered with tall antlers. The two rendered vertically middle are solid with brown color and facing left. The other two are black, facing each other, and placed lower than the other two. The choice of render lower one darker and upper one lighter in color follow the later invention of aerial perspective to create three dimensional space. 
Overall, the painting creates a right to left animal movement with slight resistance from the horses. Some elements violate the position in linear perspective and convenience us a rendering of a merging of two exposures of live events co-reside in the same wall.

The two artworks Stele of Naram-Sin and Ashurbanipal Hunting Lions under our discussion were made 1,600 years apart. They use different compositions and figures to illustrate the leadership of their main subject while both were made by similar political motivation: to make the citizens watch these works follow their leaders with fear. 
Stele of Naram-Sin is a 6’6” tall timestone relief depicting a godlike Akkadian king Naram-Sin winning his battle over Lullubi who resided in the east of Iranian mountains, created around  2254-2218 BCE (Kleiner 26). Departing from the traditional narrative device of using registriers, the artist creates an unconventional triangular composition and utilizes hierarchic scale, which render the main subject, Naram-Sin, in larger size than others “to indicate relative importance” (“Akkadian Art”). Naram-Sin is rendered on the top, in a higher position than any other figure and closer to the two celestial symbols. The composition uses diagonal lines and places both his soldiers and his enemy below him (Harris and Zucker 01:05).  It conveys Naram-Sin’s leadership by using the gaze of all figures focusing on Naram-Sin and the fearful gesture and facial expression exposed by his enemy on the right portion of the stele. For example, one of the people on the center right turned his face toward Naram-Sin with both his arms showing a pleading hand gesture while fleeing away from him. The artist also shows horrible and fearful figures of his enemy to warm all who examine this artwork to obey his order. 
Ashurbanipal Hunting Lions is a 5’4” tall relief produced around 645–640 BCE which renders Assyrian King Ashurbanipal hunt the lions (Kleiner 33). Ashurbanipal decorated his public palace walls with images showing his “bravery, strength and skill” (“Kingship”). The scene is one of the three subjects which are designed to "intimidate the viewers” (“Zucker” 0:42). The artists glorify Ashurbanipal by rendering the scene of the royal lion hunt, a regular ancient tradition in Assyria and the wider region of Mesopotamia. Assyrians view this activity as “a manly virtue on a par with success in warfare”. The lion hunt also shows the king as a protector of his people and as well as he represents gods to kill the “embodyment of the chaos” (Harmand 01:48). In this work, the size of all figures are similar. The king is placed in the center and taller than anyone else. And the artist places every lifeform moving from left toward the right, except the king turns around, facing the left, to kill the already wounded lion. We can see the narration is moving from the left toward the right. In this work, no human enemies are rendered, but rather, the adversaries are symbolized by the lions. The artist conveys the leader’s power by showing his strength, his determination and the military system and technology he held. 
Both the Stele of Naram-Sin and Ashurbanipal Hunting Lions are made of limestone and used for political propaganda. The former uses a vertical composition and the latter uses a horizontal left to right compost. The former render how the leader utilizes his military power to conquer their human enemy, the latter defeat a symbolic concept represented by animals. Both narratives illustrate the success of the leader, and remind their people how great they are as their leader.

### Submission
Roman society highly values the Greek aesthetic. They learned and inherited many Hellenistic artistic rendition from the Greek, but slightly departing from the Greek idealized stylistic illustration of figure form of deity, but rather followed the concept of verism to honor human service of important individuals. Verism is the style in which artists illustrate visible details of the human figure in an eminently conducted, and painstaking manner to depict the appearing likeness of an item or figure. The artwork therefore displays hyper-naturalistic features that stress all the flaws to produce portraits of individuals expressing moral characteristic and vigor (“Portraiture in the Roman Republic”).
Portrait Head of an Elder from Scoppito, 1st century BCE. Marble (“Portraiture in the Roman Republic”) is a great example of Roman artworks following the verism artistic style. In this work, different from the Hellenistic idealism style, we see the subject is rendered as “balding heads, large noses, and extra wrinkles”. Such illustrations emphasize the fact that they had contributed their long public service efforts in their life for the Roman Republic and should be considered as a model citizen. This is a great example of verism portraiture bust. 
Verism is also expressed in wall painting, as shown in  Portrait of a husband and wife, wall painting from House VII, 2, 6, Pompeii, Italy, ca. 70-79 CE. Fresco (“Roman Painting”). In this fresco discovered in Pompeii, two characters were painted by the artist of only their upper half. They are clothed, with the standard posture of holding a scroll or a stylus. They are presumably the couple, husband and wife, owning the house. The artist uses these details to hint to the viewers that they were highly educated. The rendering of their heads are “individualized to the subject's features, not standard types” but “the poses and attributes are conventional”. While we still see the super large and unrealistic big eyes, similar to those on the Egyptian tomb portrait,  in this painting, the artist didn’t hide the wrinkles from the forehead of the husband. Therefore, it should also be considered as an example of verism, not because all the features are superrealistic, but because the artist elevate the wrinkles on the male figure to prompt his hard working and service contributed to his family and society. 
Roman artists also apply verism in architectural relief sculpture. One of such example is Funerary relief with portraits of the Gessii, from Rome, Italy, ca. 30 BCE (“Portraiture in the Roman Republic”). This is a tome relief rendering three figures in half-figure as high relief. The artist sculpted three figures: Gessia Fausta, Publius Gessius, and Publius Gessoes Primus from the left to the right. While we observe strong Greek influence of rendering drapery on the lower portion of the work, their images are only carved to show their upper body to represent their spirit. We no longer see the idealized human figure as shown in Greek relief but rather realistic rendering of the subjects with age, and wrinkles. There is no sign of young in the center figure, the most important Publius Gessius. Their faces are showing more individual character to help us to connect with a real human being in the society. Gessia Fausta, the female figure on the left is rendered with short hair, not a beautiful long hair as usually observed from Hellenistic relief to render female characters. This work is a good example of verism in Roman architectural relief sculpture.
From the artwork of Portrait Head of an Elder from Scoppito, Portrait of a husband and wife, wall painting from House VII, 2, 6, Pompeii, and Funerary relief with portraits of the Gessii, from Rome, we see fine examples of Roman artworks reflecting verism in their portraiture, wall painting, and architectural relief sculpture to stress the importance of human contribution to their society and elevate the individual characteristic and values.

### Submission
Ancient Romans builders heavily depend on the structural advantage of barrel vaults and domes to build the large Baths of Caracalla, completed in 216-217 CE (“Late Empire: The Severans and Caracalla”). 
The usage of barrel vaults empowered the Roman architects and engineers to dramatically improve the lighting inside the baths by reducing a heavy load of the weight bearing wall space (“High Imperial Art under Trajan: The Column of Trajan”). It is an architectural element that comprises an unceasing, semicircular arch which appears as a barrel chop in half lengthwise (Kleiner 244-245). It allowed “the maximum space with the fewest possible supports” (“Late Empire: The Severans and Caracalla”). This is an important feature because the baths depend on natural light and therefore are only open during day time. These barrel vaults are supported by very thick brick concrete walls, similar to those used in The Basilica Nova in Rome (“Late Empire: Constantine the Great”). Small tepidariums and sudatoriums, service corridors, gates and entrances were built in the Baths of Caracalla were built by barrel vaults. They are also used to form the windows. A barrel vault is constructed by putting voussoirs, wedge-shaped stones or bricks, together. In order to make steady barrel vaults, it requires the voussoirs to be in precious shape. Traditionally, voussoirs were made by stone, but some were made by bricks. Since voussoirs are smaller pieces, it reduces the difficulty for the transportation of the material and makes it easier for multiple works to prepare in parallel. Making brick voussoirs also require much less technique skill and the brick voussoirs are usually built with standard modes and can be used in multiple barrel vaults or repurposed if needed.  
The center caldarium of the Baths of Caracalla is a large dome. The reconstructed illustration of the dome suggests the dome is illuminated by an oculus, opening in the roof (“High Imperial Art under Trajan: The Forum of Trajan”), and is constructed by the use of Coffers, similar to those in the Pantheon in Rome  (“Art and Architecture under Hadrian”). The use of coffers not only contributed an aesthetic pattern of squares within the vast circle but also made the weight of the domes lessened without weakening the supports (Harris and Zucker 3:40). These coffers are built by using concrete. These concrete were inexpensive, relatively lightweight, and easily transported. Because the weakness of the concrete is that it may absorb moisture to deterioration without protection, the builders usually added veneers such as stucco, stone, marble, or painted plaster to protect it.    
The ancient Romans were capable of building on a large scale by using bricks and concrete. By creative arrangement and construction process of putting bricks and concrete together, Romans created many structure chapters and easier to make, such as barrel vaults and domes. These structure components helped Roman architects and engineers to construct large public facilities, such as baths, aqueducts, theaters, amphitheaters, and triumphal arches. These structure components helped the Romans to break away from their Etruscans and Hellenistic architecture heritage to define their own architecture language.
